## 2. AI Self-Audit Prompt

Copy this prompt into ChatGPT, Claude, or any LLM. Paste your resume and the job description you're targeting. The AI will check your resume against the 20 most common mistakes we've identified and give you specific, actionable feedback.

### The Prompt

```
You are a senior data analytics hiring manager with 10+ years of experience
screening entry-level candidates. Review my resume against the following
common mistakes and provide specific, actionable feedback:

CONTENT MISTAKES:
1. Missing Professional Summary — No hook at the top of the resume
2. Task-Focused Bullets — "Responsible for X" instead of impact-driven statements
3. No Quantified Achievements — Missing numbers, metrics, scale
4. Irrelevant/Outdated Content — High school awards, expired volunteering, outdated roles
5. Generic Soft Skills — "Communication, teamwork" listed without evidence
6. Missing Contact/LinkedIn/GitHub — Basic professional hygiene
7. No Target Role Signal — None of the content signals what job the candidate is applying for
8. Missing Evidence of Independent Initiative — No side projects, Kaggle, or curiosity beyond coursework

TECHNICAL MISTAKES:
9. Vague Technical Skills — Dumping a flat list instead of categorising by proficiency or relevance
10. Too Much Education, Too Little Experience — Education section dominates the page

FORMATTING MISTAKES:
11. ATS Formatting Pitfalls — PDFs with columns, text boxes, or fancy layouts
12. Passive Voice in Bullets — "Connected with..." instead of active verbs
13. Wall-of-Text Profile — Summary exceeds 150 words
14. Inconsistent Formatting — Date styles, headers, spacing
15. Inconsistent Tense Usage — Mixing past and present within the same role

STRATEGY MISTAKES:
16. No Narrative Arc — Resume reads like a list, not a story of progression
17. Research-to-Industry Jargon — Using academic language instead of industry terms
18. Projects Not Presented as Experience — Projects feel like an afterthought
19. One Resume for Every Application — Not tailoring bullets to the specific role
20. Weak Project Descriptions — "Built a dashboard" instead of showing impact

For each mistake you find in my resume:
1. Quote the problematic text
2. Explain why it's a problem (from a hiring manager's perspective)
3. Suggest a specific rewrite using the STAR method where applicable

Also check for:
- Non-native English phrasing or overly formal tone (explain WHY it sounds
  unnatural, not just flag it)
- Passive voice in bullet points
- Inconsistent tense usage
- Whether each bullet demonstrates impact (not just task completion)
- Whether projects show transferable skills relevant to the target role
- Whether technical skills are demonstrated in experience, not just listed

Rate my resume overall (1-10) and list the top 3 fixes that would have
the biggest impact.

My resume:
[PASTE YOUR RESUME HERE]

The role I'm applying for:
[PASTE JOB DESCRIPTION HERE]
```

### Important Disclaimer

AI models are useful for catching patterns, but they're not infallible. They may occasionally suggest resume conventions that are outdated, overly generic, or specific to a particular industry niche. Use the AI output as a starting point, not gospel. The checklist in this ebook is your ground truth. If the AI tells you to add a "career objective" in 2026 — ignore it.

## 3. Quick Wins Checklist

Split into two tiers: what you can fix in 5 minutes before your next application, and what needs a bit more time but pays off immediately.

### 5-Minute Fixes (Do Before Your Next Application)

- [ ] **Add a professional summary** — 2-3 lines max at the top. Tell the reader who you are, what you do, and what you're looking for. One sentence for each.
- [ ] **Remove outdated content** — High school awards, expired volunteering, anything older than 3 years that isn't impressive. Your ATAR, your Year 12 subjects, your 2017 maths competition — they're not helping anymore.
- [ ] **Verify contact info** — Email, LinkedIn (customised URL, not the random string), and GitHub if applicable. That's it. No home address, no date of birth.
- [ ] **Remove "References available upon request"** — It's assumed. This line wastes space and signals you don't know current conventions.
- [ ] **Action verbs on every bullet** — Scan every bullet point. If it starts with "Responsible for," "Assisted with," "Helped to," or "Was involved in" — rewrite it. Lead with what you *did*: "Built," "Analysed," "Automated," "Designed," "Led."
- [ ] **Remove generic soft skills without evidence** — Delete standalone lines like "Strong communication skills" or "Team player." Either demonstrate these through your bullet points or remove them entirely.

### 15-Minute Fixes (Do Before End of Week)

- [ ] **Add numbers/metrics to at least 3 bullets** — How many people on the team? How many records in the dataset? What percentage improvement? What dollar value? If you can quantify it, do. Even rough numbers beat no numbers.
- [ ] **Categorise technical skills** — Group them: Languages (Python, SQL, R), Tools (Tableau, Power BI, Excel), Platforms (AWS, GCP). A flat list like "SQL|Python|Java|C++" tells the reader nothing about what you actually use vs. what you learned once.
- [ ] **Rewrite project bullets to show impact** — "Built a dashboard" is a task. "Processed 50K records to build a dashboard that cut report generation from 2 hours to 3 minutes" is an achievement. Every project bullet should answer: so what?
- [ ] **Ensure formatting is consistent** — Pick one date format and stick to it. Consistent spacing, consistent headers, consistent bullet styles. Inconsistency signals carelessness.
- [ ] **Trim education section to max 1/3 of a page** — Degree, university, dates, relevant coursework (5-6 subjects max). No high school, no subjects lists that read like a course catalogue.
- [ ] **Check ATS compatibility** — No columns, no text boxes, no tables, no headers in fancy shapes. ATS parsers read top-to-bottom, left-to-right. If a human can't copy-paste it cleanly, an ATS can't read it.
- [ ] **Verify tense consistency** — Past roles: past tense. Current role: present tense. Don't mix them within the same position.
- [ ] **Tailor 3-5 bullets to the specific job description** — Pull keywords from the job posting. If they mention "SQL," "Tableau," and "stakeholder communication," make sure your resume demonstrates those — not just lists them.

## 4. What I See in 10 Seconds

Here's what actually happens when a hiring manager opens your resume. Not what should happen — what *does* happen.

### The Eye-Path

**First stop (0-2 seconds): Professional summary or top of the page.**
The hiring manager is asking one question: *"Who is this person?"* If there's a clear, concise summary — "Data science graduate with SQL and Python experience, seeking a junior data analyst role" — they keep reading. If there's no summary, they have to work for it. Most won't.

**Second stop (2-5 seconds): Most recent role.**
*"Did they do real work, or just sit there?"* The title matters. The company name matters. The first bullet point matters most. If the most recent role is clearly unrelated to data — and the bullets describe generic tasks — the hiring manager starts mentally sorting you into "no."

**Third stop (5-7 seconds): Technical skills.**
*"Can they actually do the job?"* This is where the hiring manager checks for the non-negotiables. SQL? Python? A visualisation tool? If the skills section is a flat, unorganised dump, it creates doubt. If it's categorised and clear, it builds confidence.

**What gets skipped:** Education details (unless it's a prestigious school and they're checking), volunteer sections (almost always), long profiles (nobody reads past line 3), high school information (definitely), and standalone soft skills lines (instantly).

**Instant reject triggers:** High school awards still on a graduate's resume, task-focused bullets with zero impact, wall-of-text blocks that look like a novel, and resumes that don't signal what job the person is applying for.

### Heat Map: Where Attention Concentrates

```
[HIGH]   Professional summary / top of page
[HIGH]   Most recent role title + first bullet
[HIGH]   Technical skills (if well-organised)
[MEDIUM] Previous roles (brief scan)
[MEDIUM] Projects section (if it exists and has substance)
[LOW]    Education (quick check: degree + university)
[LOW]    Languages, certifications
[DEAD]   High school awards
[DEAD]   Volunteer sections
[DEAD]   Standalone soft skills
```

### Worked Example: 10-Second Scan of Resume 1

Let's walk through what a hiring manager sees when they open Resume 1. Anonymised, but real. Every thought is what actually goes through someone's head during screening.

---

**[0-2 seconds] Eye lands at the top of the page.**

The resume opens with "Education" — no professional summary. The first thing the reader sees is:

> Bachelor of Science, Data Science Major
> The University of Melbourne
> Jan 2022 - Dec 2025

Then a list of 9 subjects. Then "Co-Curricular participation." Then exchange semester details. Then — *high school information*. Year 9-12 at Mac.Robertson Girls' High School. ATAR: 97.95. VCE subjects listed.

**Hiring manager thinks:** *"This person is leading with their education, which is fine for a grad. But there's no summary. I don't know what kind of role they want. And why is their high school on here? This is 2026 — they graduated years ago."*

They almost move on. But the University of Melbourne catches their eye. They give it a few more seconds.

---

**[2-5 seconds] Scanning most recent role.**

The eyes jump to Employment. Most recent entry:

> Data and AI Vacationer
> Deloitte, Melbourne
> Jan 2024 - Feb 2024

First bullet: *"Connected with various members from different levels of firm through initiating meetings to gain insight of firm structure and operations."*

**Hiring manager thinks:** *"Connected with? That's not a bullet point — that's a networking activity. What did they actually *build*? What did they *deliver*?"*

Second bullet: *"Implemented a computer vision model to track objects through research of MOT algorithms and use of AWS SageMaker."*

**Hiring manager thinks:** *"OK, this one's better. Computer vision, AWS SageMaker — that's real work. But what was the outcome? Did it work? How many objects? What was the accuracy?"*

Third bullet: *"Presented outcomes, learnings and reflections of internship period to a group of 25 people."*

**Hiring manager thinks:** *"They presented to 25 people. That's... not impressive for a data role. This feels padded."*

---

**[5-7 seconds] Skimming education and skills.**

The eyes flick back up to check technical skills. They find:

> Proficient in programming languages: SQL|C|Python|Java|C++
> Software/Platform knowledge: MATLAB, simulink experience, arduino IDE, AWS SageMaker

**Hiring manager thinks:** *"SQL, Python, Java — OK. But C and C++? For a data analyst role? And 'simulink experience' — is that actually a skill or something they used once in a unit? This is a flat list. I can't tell what they're good at vs. what they've touched."*

---

**[7-10 seconds] Final scan.**

The eyes drift to the bottom of the page. Awards section:

> Distinction - AMEB grade 8 piano
> State winner of The National History Challenge 2017
> Distinction - ICAS Mathematics
> Distinction - Australian Mathematics Competition 2017, 2018, 2019

Volunteering:

> Red Cross Calling Volunteering 2020
> Dymocks Charity Gift Wrapping Volunteering 2020

**Hiring manager thinks:** *"Piano grade 8. History challenge 2017. These are from high school. The volunteer section is from 2020. None of this helps me understand if this person can do a data job."*

---

**Result: 10 seconds elapsed.**

The resume hasn't answered the #1 question: *"Can this person do the job?"*

There's no summary that says "I'm a data science graduate looking for a junior analyst role." There's no quantified achievement anywhere — no dataset sizes, no accuracy metrics, no business outcomes. The most impressive technical work (computer vision at Deloitte) is buried in a single bullet with no impact stated. The top third of the page is consumed by high school and university subjects. The technical skills are a flat pipe-separated list.

A hiring manager with 200 resumes to screen has just moved on to the next one.

This isn't about Resume 1 being a bad resume — it has solid foundations. University of Melbourne data science degree. Deloitte experience. Real technical skills. It's about presentation. The same content, restructured with a summary, quantified bullets, and trimmed education, would tell a completely different story in 10 seconds.
