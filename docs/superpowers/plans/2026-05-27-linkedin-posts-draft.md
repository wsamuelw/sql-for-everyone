## Post 1: AI-Native Analytics

**"How I'm using Claude Code in my analytics workflow"**

I've been building data pipelines for 15 years. Last year, I rewired how I work.

I used to spend hours writing SQL, debugging joins, building dashboards from scratch. Now I use Claude Code as a co-pilot — not to replace the thinking, but to accelerate the doing.

Here's what changed:

1. **Pipeline development** — I describe what I need in plain English. Claude writes the dbt models, the tests, the documentation. I review, adjust, ship. What used to take a day takes an hour.

2. **Exploratory analysis** — I point it at a dataset, ask it to find patterns, and it surfaces insights I'd have spent half a day uncovering manually. The analysis is still mine — the legwork isn't.

3. **Stakeholder communication** — I feed it the numbers, it drafts the narrative. I edit for tone and accuracy. Reports that used to take a morning now take 30 minutes.

The result: I'm spending 80% more time on the work that actually matters — understanding the business problem, interpreting the results, and deciding what to do next.

The tools will keep changing. The skill that won't change: knowing which questions to ask and what the answers mean for the business.

## Post 2: Enterprise Analytics at Scale

**"What managing analytics for 9M+ members taught me"**

At Flybuys, I managed campaign analytics across 9M+ active members and 20+ retail partners. Here's what I learned about building analytics at scale:

**Lesson 1: The semantic layer is everything.**

Every team had their own definition of "active member." Marketing said one thing, product said another, finance said a third. We built a semantic layer in Snowflake that unified business definitions across teams. The insight delivery improved by 20% — not because the queries got faster, but because people stopped arguing about what the numbers meant.

**Lesson 2: Self-serve is a myth without guardrails.**

Everyone wants self-serve analytics. Nobody wants to maintain it. We gave marketing teams dashboards they could explore — but with pre-built segments and validated metrics. Freedom within a framework. That's the only self-serve that actually works.

**Lesson 3: Measurement frameworks beat more data.**

We didn't need more data. We needed better measurement. Propensity score matching and marketing mix modelling let us quantify incremental uplift across 20+ partners. That's how we improved retail media revenue by 80% — not by collecting more data, but by measuring what mattered.

The scale taught me this: analytics isn't a technical problem. It's a coordination problem. The best pipelines are useless if teams don't trust the numbers.

## Post 3: Super IC / Leadership

**"Why I believe the super IC is the future of analytics"**

The analytics industry has a strange obsession with management.

Want to earn more? Manage people. Want more influence? Manage people. Want to stay technical? Good luck — the career ladder pushes you into meetings, budgets, and performance reviews.

But something's changing. AI is making individual contributors more powerful than ever.

A senior analyst with Claude Code, dbt, and Snowflake can now do what a team of 3-4 did two years ago. Write the pipeline, build the model, generate the report, draft the stakeholder communication. One person, end to end.

That's not a threat to teams. It's a threat to middle management.

The companies that get this right will create Staff and Principal IC tracks that pay like leadership roles. The ones that don't will lose their best technical people to companies that do.

I've spent 15 years in analytics — 10 of those leading teams, 5 running my own consultancy. The best work I've done wasn't when I had the biggest team. It was when I had the right tools and the freedom to use them.

The super IC isn't a consolation prize for people who don't want to manage. It's a deliberate career choice for people who want to build.

What do you think — is the super IC trend real, or just hype?

## Post 4: Mentoring / Data Culture (Bonus)

**"What I learn from mentoring 20+ data professionals"**

I mentor across Melbourne University, UNSW, RMIT, and Monash. Every cohort teaches me something new.

The biggest lesson: the gap between academic data science and industry analytics is wider than most people think.

University teaches you to build the perfect model. Industry teaches you to build the model that ships.

I tell every mentee: the query is 20% of the job. The other 80% is knowing which question to answer next, how to communicate the answer, and what to do when the answer is "we don't have enough data to know."

The mentees who do well share one trait: they're curious about the business, not just the data. They ask "why does this metric matter?" before they ask "how do I calculate it?"

That curiosity is the skill that separates a $65K analyst from a $130K strategist. And it's the skill that AI can't replace — because AI can answer questions. It can't decide which questions are worth asking.

What's the one thing you wish someone told you early in your analytics career?
